# Results

## Baseline Performance

As a reference, k-means clustering on the full (unpatchworked) data yielded an ARI of 0.185 on the raw mouse landmark dataset and 1.000 on the simulated Gaussian blobs. The low baseline ARI on the raw data reflects the inherent difficulty of separating the three valence conditions from kinematic features alone, whereas the simulated clusters are perfectly recoverable when all features are jointly observed.

## Effect of Patch Count and Sample Overlap

We swept over patch counts from 1 to 15 and sample-overlap fractions of 0%, 10%, 20%, 40%, and 80% using the sequential lstsq alignment. The results reveal two dominant trends. First, configurations with zero sample overlap universally collapsed to near-chance ARI (~0.03), confirming that shared samples are essential for alignment. Second, clustering quality degraded monotonically with increasing patch count at any fixed overlap level, as each additional alignment step compounds error along the sequential chain. The best quilting configuration (3 patches, 80% overlap) achieved an ARI of 0.205 on raw data, slightly exceeding the full-data baseline, and 0.906 on the simulated data. The worst non-trivial configuration (12 patches, 0% overlap) produced an ARI of 0.027 on raw and 0.042 on simulated data. In low-ARI regimes, 3D embedding visualizations showed points collapsing toward the origin, indicating that unaligned patches project into the null space of the accumulated subspace.

## Sequential vs Hierarchical Alignment

Across the full parameter grid (75 configurations, 5 replicates each), the sequential chain consistently outperformed the hierarchical binary-tree variant from approximately 6 patches onward, regardless of overlap level. The performance gap peaked at 10-20% overlap with 7-14 patches, where the hierarchical method's mean ARI fell 0.05 to 0.11 below the sequential method on the raw data. At very low patch counts (2-3), the two methods performed comparably because the hierarchical tree is only 1-2 levels deep and has not yet encountered its characteristic failure mode. At 80% overlap, the gap narrowed as abundant shared samples partially compensated for suboptimal merge pairing. The hierarchical method also exhibited higher variance across replicates, suggesting greater sensitivity to the specific patch geometry. On the simulated data, results were more variable: the hierarchical method occasionally won at isolated configurations (e.g., 6 patches / 80% overlap: +0.210) but also suffered catastrophic failures (e.g., 8 patches / 10% overlap: -0.208), consistent with the well-separated cluster structure amplifying both successful and failed alignments. No grid point reached statistical significance after Bonferroni correction (alpha = 0.00067) on either dataset, though several raw-data configurations approached uncorrected significance (12 patches / 20% overlap: p = 0.003; 14 patches / 40% overlap: p = 0.008), reflecting the limited power of 5 replicates.

## Failure Mode Analysis

The two alignment architectures fail in qualitatively different ways. In the sequential chain, errors compound along the O(N) alignment path: later patches are aligned against an increasingly noisy accumulated embedding, and an early bad merge propagates through all downstream steps. In the hierarchical tree, greedy overlap-maximizing pairing tends to group spatially adjacent patches into the same branch, leaving the final top-level merge to bridge two halves of the data that share few samples. This local-greediness-to-global-separation effect worsens with more patches and deeper trees.

## CCA Alignment

Replacing the unconstrained least-squares alignment with canonical correlation analysis (CCA) yielded the most notable improvement. Whereas lstsq alignment maps the incoming patch onto the anchor space in a one-sided projection, CCA computes paired projection matrices for both the accumulated embedding and the incoming patch by whitening each space and decomposing the cross-covariance. This preserves information from both input subspaces rather than discarding the component of the incoming patch that lies outside the anchor's column space. In practice, CCA-aligned embeddings retained substantially more cluster structure in the non-overlapping samples, as confirmed by higher cluster separability scores and cleaner 3D embedding visualizations relative to the lstsq baseline. This was the most impactful finding of the project: the choice of alignment method matters more than the choice of merge architecture (sequential vs hierarchical) for preserving downstream clustering quality.
